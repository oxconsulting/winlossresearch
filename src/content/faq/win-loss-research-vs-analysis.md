# Win/loss research vs. win/loss analysis?

Win/loss research is the interview process itself: independent conversations with buyers after a deal closes. Win/loss analysis is the step that follows, comparing a completed set of interviews to find patterns and convert them into findings a GTM team can act on. Research produces the raw material; analysis produces the insight.

## Where the Confusion Comes From

The two terms get used interchangeably because they're both part of the same engagement, and in casual conversation "win/loss" often covers the whole process end to end. But they describe genuinely different activities, and conflating them is how programs end up mistaking a single interview for a finding. An individual [win/loss interview](/glossary/win-loss-interview/) is one buyer's account of one deal. It's real, but it's not yet a pattern.

## What Each Step Actually Involves

[Win/loss research](/glossary/win-loss-research/) is the data collection phase: outreach to former buyers, scheduling, and conducting structured interviews built around a custom questionnaire covering the buying process, evaluation criteria, competitive comparisons, and objections. A standard program targets 20 to 30 interviews, weighted toward losses.

[Win/loss analysis](/glossary/win-loss-analysis/) is the synthesis phase: comparing that full interview set against itself for [patterns](/glossary/pattern-recognition/) that repeat across independent buyer accounts, then testing those patterns against the win interviews to see how consistently they hold. The output is an executive research report built around findings, not a transcript-by-transcript summary.

## Why the Distinction Matters in Practice

A program that runs interviews serially, one deal at a time, without ever pausing to analyze the accumulating set, is doing research without analysis. It produces a growing pile of individual deal summaries and never answers the question a leadership team is actually asking, which is what's true across the whole population of deals. Getting from research to analysis requires deliberately stepping back and comparing interviews against each other, not just collecting more of them.
