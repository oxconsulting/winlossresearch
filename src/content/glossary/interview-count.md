# Interview Count

Interview count is the number of buyer interviews conducted within a single win/loss program. A standard program targets 20 to 30 total interviews, a range chosen specifically to distinguish a reliable [pattern](/glossary/pattern-recognition/) from a single buyer's anecdote, rather than an arbitrary budget line.

## Why the Range Sits Where It Does

Below roughly 15 total interviews, a single unusual conversation can distort the entire dataset, and there typically aren't enough independent accounts to tell a genuine, recurring concern apart from something one buyer happened to mention. Above 30, additional interviews tend to confirm findings that have already emerged rather than surface new ones, so the incremental interview stops buying incremental insight.

This range also depends on where the interviews fall in the [win/loss interview](/glossary/win-loss-interview/) split. A standard program weights the count toward [loss interviews](/glossary/loss-interview/), roughly two for every one [win interview](/glossary/win-interview/), because losses tend to produce a cleaner, more specific signal than wins do.

## What a Sufficient Count Actually Does

Volume is what allows [pattern recognition](/glossary/pattern-recognition/) to function. A concern one buyer raises in an early interview is a single data point. The same concern surfacing independently, in different language, across a meaningful share of a 20-to-30-interview set is a pattern a leadership team can act on. Interview count is the variable that makes that comparison statistically meaningful in the first place.

## A Common Misunderstanding

Interview count is sometimes treated as a simple headcount target, hit by running interviews one deal at a time and stacking up individual summaries. Reaching the target this way misses the point. A program that accumulates 25 individual deal write-ups without ever comparing them against each other has technically hit its interview count and still produced no pattern-level finding. The count matters because it enables a specific comparison process, not because a number was reached.

## A Practical Example

A company sets a target of 20 loss interviews and 10 win interviews. By interview twelve, three separate buyers have independently mentioned the same onboarding concern, unprompted and in different words. That repetition, only visible because enough independent interviews existed to compare, is what turns a passing comment into a finding worth bringing to leadership.
