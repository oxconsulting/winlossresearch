# Win/Loss Research

Win/loss research is the practice of interviewing buyers shortly after they've made a purchase decision, whether they chose your product or a competitor's, to identify the real, verifiable factors that determined the outcome. It relies on structured conversations with the people who actually made the decision, not on proxies for their thinking like CRM notes, sales call recordings, or rep debriefs. The output is a set of patterns across many buyer accounts, not one deal's story.

## Why It's Distinct From Other Data Sources

Most GTM teams already believe they understand why they win and lose. That belief is usually built on the vendor's own records of the sale: what the rep logged, what the CRM field captured, what the AI summary of a call recording produced. Win/loss research starts from a different premise. The buyer is the only person with direct knowledge of the internal conversations, the competing options, and the actual reasoning that shaped the decision, and none of that reliably makes it into a system your team controls. Win/loss research goes to the source.

This distinguishes it from customer surveys and NPS, which capture a buyer's self-reported rating rather than the specific mechanism behind a decision, and from call recording analysis, which only captures what happened on calls your team was part of. A buying committee's internal deliberations happen almost entirely off camera.

## Win/Loss Research vs. Win/Loss Analysis

The two terms are related but not interchangeable. Win/loss research is the data collection process: the buyer interviews themselves. [Win/loss analysis](/glossary/win-loss-analysis/) is what happens after the interviews are complete, when individual conversations get compared against each other to find [patterns](/glossary/pattern-recognition/) that hold up across a full interview set. Research without analysis is a stack of transcripts. Analysis without enough research behind it is guesswork dressed up as a finding.

## How a Program Is Typically Structured

A standard win/loss research program targets 20 to 30 buyer interviews, split roughly two to one toward losses, and conducts them within a defined window after each deal closes. That structure exists because a single buyer's account is an anecdote. A neutral researcher hears the same competitive concern, the same demo objection, or the same internal committee dynamic repeat across a large enough set of interviews, and the pattern becomes something a leadership team can act on with confidence, not something they're taking on faith from one conversation.

## A Common Misunderstanding

Win/loss research is sometimes conflated with "asking sales why they lost a deal." That's a debrief, and it's structurally different. A rep or CS manager conducting the conversation has a relationship to protect and an interpretation already formed. A [win/loss interview](/glossary/win-loss-interview/) is conducted by someone with no stake in the outcome, which is precisely what allows a buyer to say what actually happened rather than the version that preserves the relationship.

## See It in Practice

A company logs a deal as lost to "no budget." A win/loss interview with that buyer surfaces a different account entirely: budget existed, but the internal champion lost an argument to a skeptical VP two weeks before the deal went dark, and nobody on the vendor's side was ever in that room. That detail never appears in a CRM field. It only surfaces because someone asked the buyer directly, with no sale on the line.
